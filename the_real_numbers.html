\begin{Prop} For all $a \in \mathbb{R^+}$ and $n \in \mathbb{N^{\times}}$, there is unique $x \in \mathbb{R^+}$ \\ such that x^n = a. \end{Prop} \begin{proof} Uniqueness of solution can be derived from formula which \\ already proved: \\ $a^n - b^n = (a-b)\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} a^{k}b^{n-k} > 0$ Suppose $x > a$ and have $x^n > x > a$, assume that a > 1. Next to approximate the solution \\ by declare a set that a as an upper bound: \\ $\mathbf{X} := \{ x \in \mathbb{R^+}; x^n < a \}$ Supremum of \mathbf{X} should be solution of $x^n = a$ I think, let's to \\ prove it. Let $s_n := sup(X)$ and since $\mathbb{R}$ is totally order so \\ able to prove this by assume $s^n > a$ or $s^n = a$ or $s^n < a$. \\ First to suppose that $s^n < a$. Then we can find some $\epsilon \in \mathbb{R}$ \\ such that $0 < \epsilon < (a - s^n)$, from this inequality have $s^n + \epsilon < a $. \\ Intuitively, there should be real numbers between a and $s^n$ but because s is sup(\mathbb{X}). \\ Let to prove this is a contradiction. \\ Let $b := \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n}{k} s^k$, and have $0 < \epsilon < \frac{a - s^n}{b}$ \\ able to assume $\epsilon$ is small enought so that $\epsilon < 1$. Then \\ $(s + \epsilon)^n = s^n - \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n}{k} s^k \epsilon^{n-k} \leq s^n - (\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n}{k} s^k)\epsilon < a$ which implies that $s + \epsilon \in \mathbb{X}$ \\ but which is impossible so $s^n < a$ is not true. \\ Assume that $s^n > a$. $0 < \epsilon < s^n - a$. Similarly, to reveal contradiction that some real \\ numbers between $s^n$ and a so there are some upper bound least than $s^n$. \\ Let $b := \displaystyle\sum^* \binom{n}{2j - 1} s^{2j - 1} > 0$ so $0 < \epsilon < \frac{s^n - a}{b}$ with $\epsilon \leq 1 \land s$. Thus we have \begin{equation} \begin{align*} (s^n - \epsilon) := \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} s^n (-\epsilon)^{n-k} & = s^n + \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (-1)^{n-k}s^n \epsilon^{n-k} \\ & > s^n - \displaystyle\sum^* \binom{n}{2j - 1} s^{2j - 1} \epsilon^{n - 2j + 1} \\ & > s^n - (\displaystyle\sum^* \binom{n}{2j - 1} s^{2j - 1})\epsilon \\ & > a \end{align*} \end{equation} Now let $x \in \mathbb{R^+}$ such that $x > (s^n - \epsilon)$, and from above equation we have \\ $x^n > (s^n - \epsilon) > a$ which implies that $s^n - \epsilon$ is an upper bound of $\mathbb{X}$ \\ which is impossible. Due to $\mathbb{R^+}$ is totally order so $s^n = a$. Now to deal with the case the $0 < a < 1$. Let $b := \frac{1}{a}$, and there exists $y \in \mathbb{R+}$ \\ such that $y^n = b$ then $x := \frac{1}{y}$ is the solution of $x^n = a$. \end{proof}
