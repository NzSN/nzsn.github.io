The motivation of introduce of Series is to reveal some convenient criteria.

1. Series

The first question is “What is series ?”.

Series can be defined by two equal statements:

  • Series is sequence of it’s partial sums. With definitions \[ s_{n} := \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} x_k \] the sequence \[ (s_n) \] is called series. \[ S_n \] with \[ n \in \mathbb{N} \] is partial sum of series. Hence, in the definition series is sequence of partial sums.

  • Series is an addition of infinitely of many terms.

    Series can simply written as \[ \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k \]. \[ x_k \] is called kth summand of series.

The reason why these two statements are equal is from aspect of limit of these two partial sum and limit of the infinitely addition are equal.

\[ \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} S_n = \lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} x_k = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x_k \]

2. Convergence of Series

2.1. Prop. If a series, \[ \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k \], is conveges, then the sequence of it’s summand, \[ (x_n) \], is converges too.

Proof. By assumption of this proposition we can define \[ s := \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x_k \]. By proposition, we have \[ (s_n) \] is a cauchy sequence. Then there exists \[ \epsilon > 0 \] and \[ N := N(\epsilon) \] such that

\[ | s_n - s_m | < \epsilon \] where \[ n \geq m \geq N \]

which implies that \[ | s_n - s_{n+1} | = | x_{n+1} | < \epsilon \] where \[ n > N \]

Hence, \[ (x_n) \] is a null sequence.

The converse of this proposition is not true by counter example. the harmonic series, \[ \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} \frac{1}{k} \], is not converges even though \[ \frac{1}{k} \] is a null sequence due to \[ | s_{2n} - s_n | = \displaystyle\sum_{k=n+1}^{2n} \frac{1}{k} \geq \frac{n}{2n} = \frac{1}{2} \].

3. Calculating of series

Series is a special kind of Sequence so rules apply on Convergence Sequence can be also applied to Series.

  • \[ \lim x_n + y_n = \lim x_n + \lim y_n \Rightarrow \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k + y_k = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k + \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} y_k \]
  • \[ \lim \alpha x_n = \alpha\lim x_n \Rightarrow \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} \alpha x_k = \alpha \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k \]
  • \[ \lim x_n \cdot \y_n = \lim x_n \cdot \lim y_n \Rightarrow \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k \cdot y_k = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} x_k \cdot \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} y_k \]

4. Convergence Tests

4.1. Cauchy Criterion

If for each \[ \epsilon > 0 \] there is some \[ N := N(\epsilon) \] such that \[ | s_n - s_m | = \displaystyle\sum_{k=n+1}^{m} x_k < \epsilon \] where \[ m > n \geq N \] then the series \[ (S_n) \] is converges.

4.2. Leibniz Criterion

If sequence \[ (x_n) \] is a null sequence with nonnegative terms. Then the alternating sequence \[ \displaystyle\sum_{k=0} (-1)^{k}x_k \] converges in \[ \mathbb{R} \].
