1. Polynomials

A Polynomials over R is a Formal Power Series such that almost all of term are 0.

Can be represent by identity: \[p = \sum^{n}_{i} p_i X^i\]

\begin{Prop} \[ \binom{m+n}{l} = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{l} \binom{m}{k} \binom{n}{l - k} = \displaystyle\sum_{k=0}{l} \binom{m}{l-k} \binom{n}{k} \] \end{Prop} \begin{proof} There is relation between binomial and binomial coefficient, by equation of binomial \\ and multiplication of polynomials we have \begin{equation} \begin{align*} (1+X)^{n} (1+X)^{m} & = (\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} X^{k})(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{m} \binom{m}{i} X^{i}) \\ & = \displaystyle\sum_{l}(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{l} \binom{n}{k} \binom{m}{l-k} ) X^{l} \\ \end{align*} \end{equation} Because of $(1+X)^{n}(1+X)^{m} = (1+X)^{n+m}$ so we have $$\displaystyle\sum_{l}(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{l} \binom{n}{k} \binom{m}{l-k} ) X^{l} = \displaystyle\sum_{l=0} \binom{m+n}{l} X^{l}$$ so the proposition can be obtained by two polynomials are equal only if it's coefficient match up. \end{proof}

A very important point of the equation is transformation between binomial and binomial coefficients.

  • \[(1+X)^{n} = \displaystyle\sum_{k}^{n} \binom{n}{k} X^{k}\]
  • \[(1+1)^{n} = \displaystyle\sum_{k}^{n} \binom{n}{k}\]

With Power Series you are able to transform binomial into sum of binomial coefficients.

2. Polynomial functions

Value of a polynomial \(p\) at \(x \in R\) by function: \[p(x) = \sum^{n}_{i} p_i x^i \in R\] We can use this to define a funciton which is called Polynomial function

\[\underline{p}: R \rightarrow R, x \mapsto p(x)\], where \(p \in R[X]\)

3. From Polynomial Division to Linear Factors

I don’t write down details of props and theorems in this section. Only outline of it.

\begin{Prop}[Uniqueness of Quotient] Let K be a field. Then for $p,q \in K[X]$, there are unique polynomials $s$, $r$ such that $$p = sq + r, deg(r) < deg(q)$$ \end{Prop} \begin{proof} From some concrete instance $$ p := p_0 + p_1 X^1 + p_2 X^2 $$ $$ q := q_0 + q_1 X^ 1 $$ then have $$ s_{(1)} = p_2 q_{1}^{-1} X $$ \begin{equation} \begin{align*} p - s_{(1)}q & = (p_0 + p_1 X^1 + p_2 X^2) - (p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) X + (p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_1) X^2 \\ & = p_0 + (p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) X + (p_2 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_1) X^2 \\ & = p_0 + (p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) X \end{align*} \end{equation} let $p_{(1)} = p - s_{(1)}q$ and it's degree still not least than q so we have to repeat the above process. $$ s_{(2)} = (p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1} $$ \begin{equation} \begin{align*} p_{(1)} - s_{(2)}q & = [p_0 + (p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) X] - [q0 ((p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1}) + q_1 ((p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1}) X ] \\ & = [p_0 - q0 ((p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1}))] + [(p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) - q_1 ((p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1}) X] \end{align*} \end{equation} then $$r := p_0 - q0 ((p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1}))$$ and $$ s := (p_1 - p_2 q_{1}^{-1} q_0) q_{1}^{-1} $$ \begin{equaiton} p - s_{1}q = p_{1} p_{1} - s_{2}q = p_{2} p = p_{1} + s_{1}q p = s_{2}q + s_{1}q + p_{2} \end{equation} \end{proof}

By this prop we can derive following prop

\begin{Prop}[Expanded on K] Let K be a field. $p \in K[X]$ and $a \in K$. \\ Then there exists unique $b_0, b_1, ..., b_n \in K$ such that $$ p = \displaystyle\sum_{k}^{n} b_k (X - a)^{k} = b_0 + b_1 (X - a) + b_2 (X - a)^2 + ... + b_n (X - a)^n $$ \end{Prop} \begin{proof} This can proof by induction, by *Division of Polynomials* we can derive base case: \begin{equation} p = (X - a) p_{(1)} + b_0 \end{equaiton} then to prove induction step under the induction hypothesis, $p = (X - a)^n p_{(n)} + (X - a)^{n - 1} b_{n - 1} + ... + b_0$ \\ and $deg(p_{n}) > 1$. Then so by *Division of Polynomials* again we have \begin{equation} \begin{align*} p & = [(X - a)^n ((X - a) p_{(n + 1)} + b_{n})] + .... \\ & = (X - a)^{n + 1} p_{(n + 1)} + (X - a)^n b_{n} + (X - a)^{n - 1} b_{n - 1} + ... + b_0 \end{align*} \end{equation} so the proposition is true. \end{proof} \begin{Thm}[Linear Factors] Let K be a field. $p \in K[X]$. If there is $a \in K$ is a zero of p, $p(a) = 0$. \\ Then $p$ can be expand at $(X - a)$, which divide p, as $p_1$, $deg(p) = deg(p_1) + 1$. \end{Thm} \begin{proof} Cause a is zerof of p. so $b0 = p(a) = 0$ then \[ \begin{align*} p = \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} b_k (X - a)^k = (\displaystyle\sum_{k = 0}^{n-1} b_k (X -a)^k) (X - a) \end{align*} \] \end{proof} \begin{Coro}[Consequence of Linear Factors] A nonconstant polynomial with degree m has no more than m zeros. \end{Coro}
