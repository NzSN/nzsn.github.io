2.1. Meaning of dot product of two vectors

Product of two vector is \(\cos\theta\) where \(\theta\) is angle between those two factor.

This can be prove by following:

Let \(\vec{v} := (\parallel v \parallel\cos\alpha, \parallel v \parallel \sin\alpha)\) and \(\vec{w} := (\parallel w \parallel\cos\beta, \parallel w \parallel\sin\beta)\) then

\begin{equation} \begin{align*} \vec{v} \times \vec{w} & = \parallel \vec{v} \parallel \parallel \vec{w} \parallel \cos\alpha \cos\beta + \parallel \vec{v} \parallel \parallel \vec{w} \parallel \sin\alpha \sin\beta \\ & = \parallel \vec{v} \parallel \parallel \vec{w} \parallel (\cos\alpha\cos\beta + \sin\alpha\sin\beta) \\ & = \parallel \vec{v} \parallel \parallel \vec{w} \parallel \cos(\beta - \alpha) \\ & = \parallel \vec{v} \parallel \parallel \vec{w} \parallel \cos\theta \end{align*} \end{equation}

3. Matrices

There are two views points of Matrices, Column Picture and Row Picture.

In Column Picture, matrix can be treat as merges of vectors, for example:

\[ \vec{v}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \vec{w}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \]

then have matrix which is combine of \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\). \[\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1, 1 \\ 2, 2 \\ 3, 3 \\ \end{bmatrix}\]

This is column picture of Matrix. By this way you can treat \(\mathbf{A}x\) as linear combination of vector \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{w}\).

There is another way to think of Matrix, you can think matrix is coefficents of linear equations. A 3x3 matrix is three linear equations with three unknowns for each of equations. \(\mathbf{A}x\) can be treat as dot product of each row of \(\mathbf{A}\) with \(x\)..

5. Orthogonality

Orthogonality in Linear Algebra is a property of relation between two vectors, bases, matrices. It indicate that those two vectros are perpendicular or vectors of a matrices are perpendicular with each of another matrices.

If two vector, \(\vec{v}\), \(\vec{w}\), are Orthogonal then have

\[ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = 0 \text{ and} \parallel \vec{v} \parallel + \parallel \vec{w} \parallel = \parallel \vec{v} + \vec{w} \parallel \]

By Orthogonality we are able to compute where is the point that a vector is project onto target line or target space. More than that Orthogonality can be used to comput to reason Least Squares Approximations.

Finally, we need a way to construct orthogonal vectors, a method for this purposes is called Gram-Schmidt.
