2. Structure of Lambda calculus

2.1. Lambda Term

\begin{Def}[Construction of lambda-terms] Assume that $\Lambda$ is a set of all lambda terms, and $V$ is a set of all variables. + $x \in V \Rightarrow x \in \Lambda$ (Variable Rule) + $N \in \Lambda \wedge M \in \Lambda \Rightarrow MN \in \Lambda$ (Application Rule) + $x \in V \vee M \in \Lambda \Rightarrow \lambda x. M \in \Lambda$ (Abstraction Rule) \end{Def}

2.2. Variables, Free and bound variables

Categories of variable:

  • Free: Variables that will not be bound with any concrete value.
  • Bound: Variables that not a free variable but after \(\lambda\). .
  • Binding: Variable that occurences immediately after a \(\lambda\).

we can said: abstraction of x over M binds all free occurrences of x in M ;that is why the first x in Î»x .

\begin{Def}[FV, the set of free variables of a lambda term] + $FV(x) = \{x\}$ + $FV(MN) = FV(M) \cup FV(N)$ + $FV(\lambda x. xy) = FV(xy) \cup \{x\}$ \end{Def} \begin{Def}[Closed lambda-term] A lambda term $M$ is closed if $FV(M) = \emptyset$. M also called combinator. The set of all closed lambda term is denoted by $\Lambda^{0}$. \end{Def}

3. Equality

3.1. Alpha Equivalence or Alpha Conversion

\(\alpha\text{-conversion}\) is all about conversion between two lambda that only difference is binding variable name.

\begin{Def}[Renaming Relation] Define an operation, $M^{x \rightarrow y}$ denote the result of replaceing all free occurrence of x in M by y. Renaming relation , expressed with symbol $=_{\alpha}$, is defined as follow: $\lambda x.M =_{\alpha} \lambda y. M^{x \rightarrow y}$ where $y \notin FV(M)$ and y is not a binding variable in M. \end{Def} \begin{Def}[alpha-conversion or alpha-equivalence] + (Renaming) $\lambda x. M =_{\alpha} \lambda y. M^{x \rightarrow y}$, under the same conditions in previous definition. + (Compatibility) $M =_{\alpha} N \Rightarrow ML =_{\alpha} NL, LM =_{\alpha} LN$ and $\lambda z. M =_{\alpha} \lambda z. N$ + (Reflexivity) + (Symmetry) + (Transitivity) \end{Def} \begin{Def}[alpha-convertible; alpha-equivalent; alpha-variant] $M =_{\alpha} N \Rightarrow \text{M and N are said to be alpha-convertible or alpha-equivalent}$. M is called an $\alpha$ variant of N (and vice versa). \end{Def}

4. Evaluation

4.1. Beta-Reduction

Evaluation of lambda is done by \(\beta\text{-reduction}\).

You can treat evaluation of a lambda as a collection of expressions that chained by relation \(\beta\text{-reduction}\).

Now let’s see the definition of \(\beta\text{-reduction}\).

\begin{Def}[One-step beta-reduction] + (Basis) $(\lambda x. M)N \rightarrow_{\beta} M[x := N]$ + (Compatibility) Smilar to definition of alpha-conversion. \end{Def}

In a relation, \(N \rightarrow_{\beta} M\), N is called redex (reducible expression) and M is called contractum (of the redex).

\begin{Def}[zero-or-more-step beta-reduction] $M \Rightarrow_{\beta} N$ if there exists n terms to reduction from M to N such that + $M_{0} \equiv M$ and $M_{n} \equiv N$ and + $M_{i} \rightarrow_{\beta} M_{i+1}$ where $0 \leq i \leq n$ \end{Def} \begin{Def}[beta-conversion, beta-equality] $M =_{\beta} N$ if there exists $n \geq 0$ terms such that $M_{0} \equiv M$ and $M_{n} \equiv N$ and for all i such that $0 \leq i \leq n$ have either $M_{i} \rightarrow_{\beta} M_{i+1}$ or $M_{i+1} \rightarrow_{\beta} M_{i}$ \end{Def}

4.1.1. Relation of \(\rightarrow_{\beta}\), \(\Rightarrow_{\beta}\), \(=_{\beta}\)

  • \(\Rightarrow_{\beta}\) extends \(\rightarrow_{\beta}\)
  • \(\Rightarrow_{\beta}\) is reflexive and transitive
    • (refl) \(\forall M: M \Rightarrow_{\beta} M\)
    • (trans): \(\forall L,M,N: (L \Rightarrow_{\beta} M) \wedge (M \Rightarrow_{\beta} N) \Rightarrow (L \Rightarrow_{\beta} N)\)

4.2. Normal forms and confuluence

\begin{Def}[beta-normal form; beta-nf; beta-normalising] + $M$ is in $\beta\text{-normal form}$ if $M$ does not contain any redex. + $M$ has a $\beta\text{-normal form}$ or is $\beta\text{-normalising}$, if there is an $N$ in $\beta\text{-nf}$ such that $M =_{\beta} N$. Such an $N$ is a $\beta\text{-normal form}$ of $M$ . \end{Def} \begin{Def}[Reduction Path] Reduction path is a sequence of terms that chained by $\rightarrow_{\beta}$ . if a reduction path is infinite then it has a infinite sequence. if a reduction path is finite then it has a finite sequence. \end{Def} \begin{Def}[Weak normalisation, strong normalisation] + M is weakly normalising if it has a $\beta\text{-normal form}$ . + M is storngly normalising if there are no infinite reduction paths starting from M . \end{Def} \begin{Thm}[Church-Rosser] $(M \Rightarrow_{\beta} N_{1}, M \Rightarrow_{\beta} N_{2}) \Rightarrow (\exists L, N_{1} \Rightarrow_{\beta} L, N_{2} \Rightarrow_{\beta} L)$ \end{Thm} \begin{Coro} If $M =_{\beta} N$ then there exists $L$ such that $M \Rightarrow_{\beta} L, N \Rightarrow_{\beta} L$. \end{Coro} \begin{Lemma} + If M has $N$ as $\beta\text{-normal form}$ then $M \Rightarrow_{\beta} N$ + A $\lambda\text{-term}$ has at most one $\beta\text{-normal form}$ \end{Lemma}
